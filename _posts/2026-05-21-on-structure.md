*This is a placeholder — the first note in an ongoing series.*

Structure is not a property of data. It is a relationship between a representation and a question.

When we say that a tissue slide "has structure," we mean that there exists some map — some encoding — under which the geometry of biological organization becomes legible. The stain reveals. The embedding isolates. The graph collapses distance into meaning.

What strikes me is that this is always relational. No dataset is structured in the abstract. It becomes structured *with respect to* a query, a task, a way of looking.

Topology formalizes this intuition. Persistent homology, for instance, does not tell you what shape a point cloud is — it tells you what shapes *persist* across scales of measurement. The invariants that survive. The features that refuse to disappear.

That is, perhaps, what representation learning is really after: not features, but invariants.
